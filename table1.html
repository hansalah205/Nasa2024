1. Classification by Size and Composition Gas Giants.

Type Description Characteristics Examples
Hot Jupiters Large gas giants orbiting close to their stars Extreme heat, massive size HD 209458b, 51 Pegasi b
Super-Earths Rocky planets larger than Earth but smaller than Neptune Potentially habitable, diverse compositions Kepler-452b, Gliese 667 Cc
Mini-Neptunes Gas-rich planets smaller than Neptune Atmospheric diversity, possible moons Kepler-11b, HD 40307g
Earth-like Planets Rocky planets with conditions similar to those of Earth Habitable zones, potential biosignatures Proxima b, TRAPPIST-1e
Ice Giants Cold gas giants composed primarily of water, ammonia, and methane ices Low temperatures, high pressures Uranus, Neptune
Gas Giants Large planets composed primarily of hydrogen and helium gases Massive size, atmospheric storm systems Jupiter, Saturn
Terrestrial Planets Rocky planets with solid surfaces Diverse geology, potential habitability Earth, Mars
Neptune-like Planets Cold gas giants with compositions similar to Neptune's Low temperatures, high pressures Neptune, HD 209458b

2. Exoplanet Temperature Categories.

Category Description Characteristics Examples
Hot Exoplanets (Hot Jupiters) Planets with very short orbital periods, located close to their host stars Extremely high surface temperatures due to proximity to star 51 Pegasi b
Warm Exoplanets Planets in or near the habitable zone with moderate temperatures Temperatures allowing for diverse atmospheric and surface conditions Kepler-452b, TRAPPIST-1e
Cold Exoplanets Planets far from their stars with very low surface temperatures Similar to or colder than Neptune/Uranus, with long orbital periods and icy surfaces Neptune, Uranus, HD 209458b

3. Classification of Exoplanets by Orbital Characteristics.

Category Description Characteristics Examples
Circumbinary Planets Planets orbiting around two stars instead of one Unique stability challenges, potential for diverse environments Kepler-16b
Rogue Planets Planets not gravitationally bound to any star, drifting freely Formed in planetary systems, ejected due to gravitational interactions
Pulsar Planets Planets orbiting highly magnetized, rapidly rotating neutron stars (pulsars) Extreme environments due to intense radiation PSR B1257+12 system
Tidally Locked Planets Planets with permanent day and night sides due to tidal locking Stark temperature contrasts, affecting atmospheric dynamics and habitability

4. Classification of Exoplanets by Location Relative to the Star.

Category Description Characteristics Examples
Inner Planets (Hot Zone) Planets very close to their star, within the "hot zone" High surface temperatures, rapid orbital periods, harsh environments HD 209458b, 51 Pegasi b
Habitable Zone Planets Planets in the "Goldilocks Zone" where liquid water can exist Moderate temperatures, potential for life, prime candidates for habitability studies Kepler-452b, TRAPPIST-1e
Outer Planets (Cold Zone) Planets far from their star, beyond the habitable zone, in the "cold zone" Very low temperatures, slow orbital periods, icy or frozen gas-covered surfaces Uranus, Neptune, HD 40307g

5. Classification of Exoplanets by Formation and Atmospheric Characteristics.

Category Description Characteristics Examples
Water Worlds Planets covered by deep global oceans Thick atmospheres, lack of solid land surface, potential subsurface oceans Kepler-22b, Gliese 1214 b
Carbon Planets Planets with carbon-dominated surface composition Surfaces covered with carbon compounds (graphite, diamonds), exotic landscapes Hypothetical (theoretical models)
Lava Worlds Planets with extremely high surface temperatures Molten lava surfaces, harsh environments, volcanic activity 55 Cancri e, Kepler-10b

6. Classification of Exoplanets by Atmospheric Composition.

Category Description Characteristics Examples
Hydrogen-rich Atmosphere Planets Thick atmospheres dominated by hydrogen and helium Deep and dense atmospheres, strong greenhouse effects Jupiter, Saturn, HD 209458b
Thick Atmosphere Planets Dense atmospheres with gases like CO2, CH4, or NH3 High surface pressure, dynamic weather systems, cloud formations Venus, Titan, Kepler-11b
Thin Atmosphere Planets Very thin atmospheres, similar to Mars Weak atmospheric pressure, barren/rocky surfaces, low temperatures Mars, TRAPPIST-1b, Kepler-10b
